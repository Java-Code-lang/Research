Scenario Description

Harsh, a middle-class 19-year-old from India, took a 20 lakh INR loan at 11% interest for 120 months and a 6 lakh INR loan at 5% to study in Canada for 16 months. The plan was to work 50 hours/week, cover $1,000 CAD/month living costs, save the rest, secure Permanent Residency (PR), get a high-paying job, and start a business or diversify wealth. However, rising racism, hate crimes, and crime made life unsafe, with constant fear. Savings were only ~40,000 INR/month, far below expectations. Harsh returned to India with ~26 lakh INR debt, an unused 3-year work visa, and no savings.

Mistakes Made

  • • Ignored known risks: Dismissed news of racism and job scarcity, assuming personal success despite evidence.
  • • Overleveraged debt: Took 26 lakh INR in loans (11% and 5% interest) without a fallback, risking ~2.5 lakh INR/year in interest.
  • • Overoptimistic savings: Expected high savings post-$1k CAD/month costs, but job challenges limited savings to ~40k INR/month.
  • • No PR contingency: Relied on quick PR without alternatives, despite high rejection rates (80% for Indians).
  • • Ignored mental health: Didn’t anticipate trauma from hate crimes, impacting productivity.
  • • Refused to pivot: Stayed despite job after 1 month and warnings, driven by pressure and overconfidence.
  • • Failed to research: Daydreamed during 5-month prep instead of researching PR difficulties and social risks.

Risk to Reward Analysis

Initial Ratio: ~1:4. Risked ~35-40 lakh INR (26 lakh debt + ~2.5 lakh/year interest + 5-7 lakh opportunity cost) for potential rewards of ~50 lakh INR/year post-PR, with 100-200 lakh INR wealth in 5-10 years via business/investments. Required quick PR and stable conditions.

Actual Outcome: ~1:0.2 or worse. Ignored risks (racism, job scarcity) and relied on luck for PR (80% rejection rate). Savings were only ~40k INR/month, returned with ~30 lakh INR debt, no PR, and unused visa. Overconfidence, external pressures, and no research turned it into a gamble with near-zero reward.

How Bad It Was for Harsh

Devastating: Returned with ~30 lakh INR debt, no savings, and lost 16 months’ earnings (~5-7 lakh INR). Trauma from hate crimes caused emotional distress, reducing productivity. Unused 3-year visa meant missed career opportunities, setting back wealth-building by 3-5 years, with high interest crippling finances.

What to Do Next to Build Wealth

  • • Debt Repayment: Prioritize 11% loan with extra payments from side gigs (e.g., freelancing) to cut interest.
  • • Emergency Fund: Save 3-6 months’ expenses in a high-yield account before investing.
  • • Upskill: Use Canadian degree for better Indian/remote jobs, targeting 20-30% salary hike.
  • • Invest Smart: Start mutual funds/SIPs with 1:3 risk-reward, aiming for 12-15% returns over 5 years.
  • • Side Hustle: Freelance or start low-risk online business (e.g., tutoring) for 1:2+ returns.
  • • Mental Health: Seek therapy for trauma; plan finances with contingencies, track monthly.

Assessment of Harsh's Decision-Making: Smart, Dumb, or a Trap?

  • • Smart or Dumb?: Dumb. Overconfidence ("I can manage while others can’t") and ignoring known risks (racism, job scarcity) made it a reckless gamble, not a calculated move.
  • • 19-Year-Old Mindset?: Typical 19-year-olds lack the experience for such high-stakes decisions. Harsh’s hubris and belief in outperforming others reflect youthful overconfidence, not mature judgment.
  • • A Trap?: Yes, marketed as a “Canadian dream” by agents/colleges, hiding 80% PR rejection rates and policy shifts. It preys on middle-class aspirations, profiting from debt-laden students.
  • • Luck-Based?: Initially calculated (1:4 ratio), but success relied on luck (stable policies, safety). Ignored news of racism and job issues turned it into a gamble.
  • • External Pressures: Refused to return after 1-month job due to peer pressure (“only rich succeed,” “things will change”), fueling stubbornness despite risks.
  • • Blunder of Daydreaming: Had 5 months to research but daydreamed, missing critical data on PR difficulties and hate crimes, a major error that sealed the trap.

Verdict

The initial 1:4 risk-to-reward ratio was theoretically appealing but became a ~1:0.2 gamble due to Harsh’s failure to address known risks and reliance on luck. The decision was dumb, exacerbated by a trap-like system and a 19-year-old’s immature mindset, leading to a near-total loss scenario.

Scenario Description

Harsh repaired two phones (worth ~20,000 INR combined): a OnePlus with a screen issue and an Oppo A3s with a charging issue, in a hometown with many repair shops. For the OnePlus, a local shop warned the previous duplicate screen would fail, offering an original screen for 7,000 INR or duplicates for 1,600 INR and 4,000 INR. Knowing local repairers were often unskilled, Harsh found an LCD screen online for 900 INR + 50 INR delivery (avoiding COD at 250 INR extra due to seller distrust). He hesitated when the delivery guy refused to open the box, contacted the seller to express distrust, but bought it after seller pushback. The screen worked. He paid a local shop 100 INR to open and 100 INR to reassemble the OnePlus. For the Oppo A3s, he paid 350 INR for a charging repair, suspecting minimal work (possibly just the jack). The shop claimed the Oppo A3s battery was dead, needing replacement (1,000-2,000 INR), but Harsh fixed it at home, knowing it was discharged from 15 days of non-use. Due to laziness, he didn’t buy a hot air gun (550 INR) and glue stick (50 INR), which could have enabled him to do all repairs himself, saving the 550 INR spent on labor and charging repair.

Risk to Reward Analysis

OnePlus Screen Repair Risks: Risked 1,150 INR (900 INR screen + 50 INR delivery + 200 INR labor) on a potentially faulty screen, delivery disputes, and unskilled labor. Compared to the shop’s 1,600 INR duplicate screen, risked 1,150 INR for 450 INR savings (or 5,850 INR vs. 7,000 INR original).

Oppo A3s Charging Repair Risks: Paid 350 INR for possibly unnecessary work (suspected scam). Avoided 1,000-2,000 INR battery replacement by fixing the discharged battery at home.

Total Risk to Reward Ratio: OnePlus: ~1:0.4 (1,150 INR for 450 INR savings vs. 1,600 INR) or ~1:5 (vs. 7,000 INR). Oppo A3s: ~1:3-6 (350 INR for 1,000-2,000 INR saved). Combined: ~1:2-4 (1,500 INR risked for 1,450-7,850 INR saved). Had Harsh bought tools (600 INR), he could have saved 550 INR labor, improving the ratio to ~1:3-5 (950 INR for 2,000-8,400 INR saved).

Assessment: How Smart or Dumb Was Harsh?

  • • Smart Aspects: Sourced a 900 INR screen, saving 450-5,850 INR vs. shop prices. Fixed Oppo A3s battery at home, saving 1,000-2,000 INR. Used local labor (200 INR) to avoid tool costs, preserving the 20,000 INR phone value.
  • • Dumb Aspects: Paid 350 INR for possibly unnecessary Oppo A3s charging repair without challenging the shop. Ordered screen despite distrust, risking issues. Due to laziness, didn’t buy a hot air gun (550 INR) and glue stick (50 INR), missing a chance to save 550 INR labor.
  • • Street-Smart Potential: Borrowing a friend’s tools could have reduced costs to 950 INR, saving 550 INR more, leveraging local knowledge (unskilled shops) and connections in a town with many repair shops.
  • • Capitalist Potential: A capitalist would buy tools (600 INR) to repair phones for profit, leveraging the town’s repair shops as a market. Laziness in not buying tools kept Harsh from this scalable approach.
  • • Overall: Street-smart but limited by laziness. Harsh’s repairs were smart (1:2-4 ratio), but not buying tools (600 INR) for 550 INR savings missed a chance to act more like a capitalist, staying closer to frugal, middle-class street-smarts.

His DIY Oppo A3s battery fix was savvy, but laziness in not buying tools (600 INR) missed 550 INR savings and a capitalist opportunity to profit in a town with many shops.
